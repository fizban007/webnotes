Experiments on Weak Pulsars

1 Rationale

I believe that the biggest difference between realistic pulsars and our simulations is the scale separation. The maximum acceleration potential for the pulsar polar cap can be computed as follows:

\[ \gamma_\mathrm{max} \sim \frac{1}{4}\frac{R_{*}}{R_\mathrm{LC}}\left( \frac{R_{*}}{\lambda_p} \right)^2 \approx \mu_B\Omega^2 \]

which can be as large as \(\sim 10^9\) for realistic parameters, while secondary pairs only have Lorentz factors of \(\gamma_s\sim 10^{2-3}\). In the simulation done in 2014, \(\gamma_\mathrm{max} \sim 250\) only, and secondaries have \(\gamma_s\sim 10\). Even if all primary particles tap the full polar cap potential and convert it into pairs, the multiplicity can only achieve \(\sim 25\) which might not be enough to support a force-free magnetosphere. Increasing this ratio might be the best way to approach realistic weak pulsars.

2 Comparison between different voltage ratios

The "Type-II" solution we got in 2014 had the following parameters:

Resolution \(512\times 512\)
\(\mu\) \(2.5\times 10^4\)
\(\gamma_\mathrm{thr}\) \(\sim 20\)
\(R_\mathrm{LC}/R_*\) \(10\)
\(R_\mathrm{cutoff}\) \(\sim 4\)
\(\gamma_\mathrm{max}/\gamma_{thr}\) \(10\)

Note that we use an almost instant pair creation scheme, where \(\ell_{ph}\) is only several timesteps. The energy of secondary pairs is just a fixed fraction of the threshold, therefore we use the ratios \(\gamma_\mathrm{max}/\gamma_s\) and \(\gamma_\mathrm{max}/\gamma_\mathrm{thr}\) interchangeably. The reality can be more complicated. Because the first generation of secondaries can emit synchrotron radiation that further cascades into generations of pairs, \(\gamma_\mathrm{max}/\gamma_s\) can be significantly larger than \(\gamma_\mathrm{max}/\gamma_\mathrm{thr}\).


I carried out a series of simulations that have increasingly higher voltage ratios. Here is a comparison video of the charge densities normalized to \(\rho_\mathrm{GJ}\) of the respective case:

Time in the video is measured in units of \(R_{*}/c\), and the video runs for \(2\) rotations in total. The four cases shown in the above video have the following common parameters:

Resolution \(2048\times 2048\)
\(\gamma_\mathrm{thr}\) \(25\)
\(R_\mathrm{LC}/R_{*}\) \(8\)
\(R_\mathrm{cutoff}\) \(3\)

Both GR effect and some effective synchrotron cooling are included. The compactness parameter \(r_g/R_{*}\) is taken to be \(0.5\), and synchrotron cooling damps the perpendicular momentum of any leptons in \(10\) timesteps at the pole of the star. The four cases differ by their \(B\) field strength at the equator (\(\mu_B\) to be more accurate), which corrspond to voltage ratios as follows:

\(B_0/(m_ec^{2}/eR_{*})\) \(\gamma_\mathrm{max}/\gamma_\mathrm{thr}\)
\(4\times 10^4\) 25
\(8\times 10^4\) 50
\(12\times 10^4\) 75
\(16\times 10^4\) 100
\(24\times 10^4\) 150

A clear trend can be seen from the video. When the voltage ratio is low, the pulsar can barely sustain pair creation, let alone forming a Y-point. The system settles down to some electrosphere-like solution, which was dubbed "Type-II" in our 2014 paper. With increased voltage ratio, a continuum of different types of solutions appear. At 4 times larger voltage ratio, the system becomes very close to a force-free state, with a sustained Y-point and current sheets. As can be seen later, the spin-down power is also very close to the force-free value \(L_0 = \mu^2\Omega^4\).


The intermediate states are interesting. A current sheet can barely form between the Y-point and the star, around the last closed field line. Pair creation is ignited episodically, launching a quasi-neutral stream of plasma towards the Y-point when it is active. The electrons in this quasi-neutral plasma stream later fall back towards the star to trigger the next episode of pair creation.


Below is a plot of total integrated Poynting flux at the star for the different cases:

The kind of oscillations from pair creation episodes are very evident in the light curves. There is one more case shown here which has \(B\) field and voltage ratio even higher than the ones listed above. The spin down power is closest to the force-free value for this case.


At this point, I believe this demonstrates that for realistic pulsars, even those that cannot support pair creation through \(\gamma\gamma\) collision at the light cylinder, should be well-described by the force-free solution.

3 Robustness of the Results

This section is for understanding the nuances of the results, and compare how different physical effects affect the simulation.

3.1 Cooling vs. No Cooling

Initially I was not putting in synchrotron cooling, and it was surprisingly easy to get a working pulsar. For example, at \(B_0 = 8\times 10^4\) I already have a quasi-steady magnetosphere. A movie that summarizes the difference at \(B_0 = 8\times 10^4\) is shown here:

The case of no cooling shows a much more steady Y-point and return current sheet. It also has been run longer and it becomes even more steady afterwards. I think what is happening is that since particles can easily magnetic bottle in the return current region, the initial electron cloud near the Y-point will simply not deplete, but bounce back and forth between the star and the light cylinder. This makes it much easier to conduct the return current, therefore stabilizing the whole magnetospheric structure.


To get an idea of the effect of magnetic bottling, a simple experiment was done with some test particles. I put a layer of particles at \(r = 2R_{*}\) with momentum \(p_r = -10\), and let them fall towards the star, without evolving \(E\) or \(B\) fields. Without cooling, they do this:

Except for particles within a small angle from the axis, all particles simply bounce around the field lines, and never touch the surface. For typical cooling parameters I was using in the above simulations, they do this instead:

The bottling effect is significantly reduced, and all particles quickly lose their perpendicular momentum and fall into the star.


The cooling I'm using in the simulations is simply damping out perpendicular momentum \(p_{\perp}\). In other words, at every timestep, I simply set \(p_{\perp}\) to be \((1 - \eta)p_{\perp}\) where \(\eta\) depends on local \(B\) field, and is \(0.1\) at the pole of the star. The drawback for this method is that it kills corotation too. But in reality I'm observing that this effect is negligible, and particles quickly start to corotate with the magnetosphere once they are extracted from the surface.


The alternative is to use the full Landau-Lifshitz synchrotron cooling formula. The good thing is that it respects \(E\times B\) drift, therefore preserves corotation, but there are also drawbacks to this method. Firstly it introduces numerical instabilities when \(B\) field is high and gyration is not fully resolved. It is fine when particles near the star have negligible pitch angles, but any particle with large pitch angle will soon see its momentum go to infinity in several timesteps. This is difficult to prevent and will unpredictably crash the simulation. Secondly, the formula is only valid when the Lorentz factor of the particles is much larger than 1. However, if one sets an artificial lower bound on \(\gamma\) below which we stop cooling, then it is even easier to create the scenario in the first case where a particle at large \(B\) field has significant pitch angle (with marginal Lorentz factor). Overall I find this approach to be unpredictable and prone to crash. When it works, the simulation results are very similar to simply killing \(p_{\perp}\), therefore I simply decided to use the previous scheme.

3.3 The Effect of GR

A run without GR is done with \(B_0 = 16\times 10^4\), and other parameters the same as previous runs. I also had to inject more particles from the surface so that injection alone can support GJ current. The results is the follows:

As one can see, the polar cap is almost completely quiet, while the rest of the magnetosphere is Forcefree-like. The only pair creation site is the return current sheet. There is some spurious pair creation near the equator in the closed field line zone, but it is dynamically unimportant (probably it is either due to cooling scheme, or a bug in injection). This makes sense, since GR effect is known to not change global current flow (Phillippov et. al. 2015), but only modify local \(\rho_\mathrm{GJ}\) at the polar cap. It will make the polar cap active, but otherwise not affect the structure of the magnetosphere.


A control experiment with the same injection parameters was done to compare with the above case. Here is a video of comparison:

The top two plots show agin charge density, and lower two plots show pair creation rate. The two cases are almost indistinguishable in terms of global magnetic field structure or charge structure, but notably there is pair creation activity above the polar cap in the GR case. This is in agreement with Sasha's paper.


One interesting thing I noticed when doing the GR comparison is that the total Poynting flux near the star seems to be changed by the GR effect. If we define the spindown as the integrated Poynting flux at the stellar surface:

\[ L = 2\pi\int_0^{\pi}(E_{\theta}B_{\phi} - E_{\phi}B_{\theta})R_{*}^2\sin\theta\,d\theta \]

then we seem to get total Poynting flux at the light cylinder to be higher than at the stellar surface. This would apparently break energy conservation. However, if one multiply the stellar flux by a factor of 1.25, which is the factor change in \(\rho_\mathrm{GJ}\), then energy conservation seems restored.

This issue does not show up when there is no GR. The total Poynting flux at the star is always higher than at the LC after the initial transient. The total dissipation at the LC is approximately 10% only.
